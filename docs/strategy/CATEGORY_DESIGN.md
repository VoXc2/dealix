## Context
Dealix does not compete inside existing AI agency / chatbot / automation
agency categories. Those categories sell tools; Dealix sells an
operating model. This file is the category-design argument: what the
old category is, why it fails, what new category Dealix proposes, and
the public language we use. It pairs with the Arabic category narrative
in `docs/strategic/CATEGORY_DESIGN_AND_MASTER_OPERATING_SYSTEM_AR.md`,
the executive summary in
`docs/strategic/DEALIX_CATEGORY_EXECUTIVE_SUMMARY_EN.md`, and the
positioning in `docs/COMPETITIVE_POSITIONING.md`.

## Old Category
The dominant offer in the Saudi/MENA market today is:

- **AI agency.** Builds a chatbot or assistant; ships and disappears.
- **Automation agency.** Wires up tools; the integration breaks when
  data changes.
- **Chatbot provider.** Sells a script or template; quality drifts
  without governance.

These vendors sell tools, not a way to operate.

## Why the Old Category Fails
- Tools without an operating model do not change how the business
  decides, governs, or measures.
- AI quality decays without evals; nothing in the old category provides
  them.
- Governance, PII, and approvals are treated as paperwork, not as
  runtime behaviour.
- Buyers cannot tell which tool to use first; the diagnostic step is
  missing.

## New Category — AI Operations Partner
Dealix proposes a new category: **AI Operations Partner**. The buyer
is hiring not a tool but a partner who:

- Operates AI as a capability with measurable levels.
- Runs governance, evals, and proof at runtime.
- Decides what to build and what not to build.
- Reports back in management language.

## Point of View
The Dealix point of view is one sentence:

> AI is not a feature; AI is an operating capability.

The corollary, used in sales and content, is:

> AI succeeds not because it is smart; AI succeeds when it enters a
> clear process, with ready data, approvals, quality, and measured
> impact.

## New Belief
- AI only creates value when it is embedded into data, workflows,
  governance, and proof systems.
- Implementation without operating model produces failed pilots.
- Operating model without capability levels produces feature drift.
- Capability levels without evals produce silent quality decay.

## Public Language
- **Headlines:** "Make AI operate inside your company, not next to
  it."
- **Subheads:** "Capability-first. Governance at runtime. Proof on
  delivery."
- **Anti-jargon:** we avoid "AI-powered", "AI-driven", "AI assistant",
  except when used to describe the buyer's current tool, not the
  Dealix offer.

## Category Education Surfaces
- **Sales:** AI Ops Diagnostic frames the buyer's problem in the new
  category.
- **Content:** every public post anchors to one of the seven
  capabilities.
- **Pricing:** the offer ladder is shaped by capability levels, not by
  features or hours.
- **Proof:** every proof pack frames outcomes in capability uplift
  language.

## Anti-patterns to Reject
- Building a chatbot without a Knowledge Capability roadmap.
- Selling an automation without an Operations Capability level target.
- Promising "AI revenue" without a Revenue Capability level transition.
- Building anything Level-5 without Governance Capability at ≥ 2.

## Metrics
- **Category Recall** — share of mid-market buyers in the pipeline who
  recognise "AI Operations Partner" as a category (track, target
  rising).
- **Win Reason — Category** — share of wins where the buyer cited the
  category framing as the reason (target ≥ 30%).
- **Anti-pattern Refusals** — count of deals declined because they
  violated category anti-patterns (track, not bound).
- **Content Anchoring** — share of public content explicitly anchored
  to one of the seven capabilities (target ≥ 90%).
