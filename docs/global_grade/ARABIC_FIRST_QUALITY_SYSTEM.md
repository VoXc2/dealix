# Arabic-First Quality System

Dealix does not “support Arabic.” Dealix is **Arabic-native** in business voice, with English as a parallel surface. Arabic is a strategic asset, not a localization afterthought.

## 1. The QA rubric for Arabic outputs

Every Arabic output is scored on:

- **Clarity** — short sentences, no English transliteration soup.
- **Executive tone** — appropriate for senior buyers.
- **Saudi business fit** — domain words and formulations that ring true locally.
- **No exaggeration** — no guaranteed-outcome language.
- **No literal translation** — outputs are authored in Arabic, not back-translated.
- **No guaranteed claims** — only claims that ladder to a Proof Pack ID.
- **Actionability** — the next step is explicit.

Outputs below threshold are sent back to the operator, not the client.

## 2. Forbidden language

- نضمن لك مبيعات
- نجيب لك عملاء مؤكدين
- أتمتة واتساب باردة
- سكربنج شامل
- أقوى AI في العالم

## 3. Replacement language

- نرتب الفرص
- نقيس الأثر
- نجهز مخرجات قابلة للمراجعة
- نخفض المخاطر
- ننتج Proof Pack
- نرفع Capability Score

## 4. Why Arabic quality is a moat

- Arabic is the language of buyer decisions in this region.
- Buyers reject AI outputs that read like translation.
- Regulators and senior leadership want disclosures in Arabic alongside English.
- The market’s Arabic AI quality bar is rising — Dealix raises it further by making this a measured QA dimension.

## 5. Operating discipline

- Every BU has at least one Arabic-native voice reviewer.
- The QA rubric is applied to every client-facing output, not just marketing.
- Arabic and English are produced in parallel; neither is derivative.
- Governance disclosures default to Arabic + English on equal footing.

## 6. Productization

- A reusable **Arabic Style Guide** lives in the Capital Ledger.
- A **forbidden phrases** rule pack is enforced by the governance runtime.
- An **Arabic outreach pattern library** is maintained per sector.
- Academy tracks include an Arabic business writing capstone.

## 7. Failure modes

- Translating English drafts and calling it Arabic.
- Letting marketing language slip into client outputs.
- Treating Arabic quality as a marketing concern, not a delivery concern.
- Marketing automation outputs reaching customers without governance.
