# Saudi Data Intelligence

Saudi Arabia exposes a rich layer of open and licensed data. Dealix uses this layer to power **market intelligence, benchmarks, and sector context** — under strict governance.

## 1. What Dealix uses it for

- Market intelligence
- Sector analysis
- City / region insights
- Benchmark context
- Public data enrichment for ICP construction

## 2. What Dealix never does with it

- Mix licensed public data with a client’s private records.
- Use PII from sources whose allowed-use is unclear.
- Build outreach campaigns on data that does not permit it.
- Claim accuracy beyond what the underlying dataset supports.
- Republish source data verbatim outside its license.

## 3. Governance posture

Every public dataset enters Dealix only through a **Source Passport**:

- Provenance and owner
- License + allowed use
- Sensitivity classification
- Residency
- Refresh cadence
- Last reviewed date

Datasets without a complete passport cannot be invoked by any agent.

## 4. Productized outputs

Public-data work compounds into productized assets such as:

- Saudi B2B AI Readiness Benchmark
- Saudi SME Data Readiness Report
- Revenue Intelligence Benchmark by Sector
- AI Governance Gap Report

These become authority + inbound + partner interest — never raw scraping outputs.

## 5. Operating discipline

- Every benchmark report ships with **a method appendix** that names every dataset, license, and limitation.
- Every chart is reproducible from the underlying records.
- Every claim sources to a row in the Proof Library.
- Every report is reviewed by an Arabic-native voice before publication.

## 6. Why this is a strategic asset

- It turns public data into Dealix’s point of view on the market.
- It seeds inbound Capability Diagnostic requests.
- It gives partners and academy participants a shared baseline.
- It makes Dealix legible to regulators and policy makers without compromising buyers.

## 7. Failure modes

- Treating open data as a free resource without licensing review.
- Publishing benchmarks without a method appendix.
- Mixing private engagement data into public benchmarks without explicit consent and aggregation.
- Letting reports drift out of date — every benchmark must have an expiry.
