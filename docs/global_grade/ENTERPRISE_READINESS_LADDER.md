# Enterprise Readiness Ladder

The Enterprise Readiness Ladder describes the maturity Dealix needs to *sell* a given enterprise tier. **Sell what you can operate**, not what looks impressive.

## 1. Level 1 — Trust Pack

- Documented data handling
- AI policy in writing
- No unsafe automation in any offer
- Proof Pack format defined and used

A buyer at Level 1 receives the Trust Pack as the trust artifact.

## 2. Level 2 — Auditability

- Structured audit events
- AI Run Ledger active
- Approval records persisted
- Source Passports for every dataset in flight

A buyer at Level 2 receives a quarterly Audit Export.

## 3. Level 3 — Control

- Policy engine deployed
- Role-based approvals
- Risk index per agent
- Incident response runbook

A buyer at Level 3 receives a Governance Pack with rule definitions and the Risk Index dashboard.

## 4. Level 4 — Enterprise Platform

- RBAC
- SSO
- Configurable data retention
- Audit exports
- SLA on uptime and approval latency
- Defined support process

A buyer at Level 4 receives a formal enterprise contract with the Platform addenda.

## 5. Level 5 — Enterprise AI OS

- AI Control Tower
- Multi-workflow governance
- Business-unit reporting
- Executive value dashboard

A buyer at Level 5 receives the Governed AI Operations Program, with a joint operating committee.

## 6. Rules

- Dealix does **not** sell Level N if it cannot operate Level N–1.
- Every level has explicit operational evidence to prove it.
- The full ladder is designed up front so capability deepens without rework.
- The ladder applies to the firm and to each Business Unit independently.

## 7. Failure modes

- Selling Level 5 features when the firm operates at Level 2.
- Letting the ladder become aspirational only — every level must have a real artifact.
- Conflating Level 4 platform features with Level 5 control tower scope.
- Skipping the audit export discipline that Level 2 demands.

## 8. Why this exists

Buyers are willing to start at Level 1 and grow with Dealix when the path is explicit. Buyers walk away when the firm overpromises capabilities it has not operated.
